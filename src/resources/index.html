Related resources

There’s no shortage of research on the potential extent and impacts of sea level rise in the Bay Area. Here’s a cross-section of what is available online.

The Risk
Want to see how rising water levels might shrink your city? Check out this mapping tool from Climate Central: “Surging Seas: Risk Zone Map"
The most thorough look at how and where regional marshes might be restored is the fall 2015 report published by the California Coastal Conservancy “The Baylands and Climate Change: What We Can Do”
The Bay Conservation and Development Commission’s efforts to begin analyzing the potential impacts of sea level rise on specific communities can be found at "Adapting to Rising Tides"
The start of San Francisco’s efforts to begin preparing for water-related aspects of climate change is the March 2016 “Sea Level Rise Action Plan”
A recent vulnerability assessment for portions of San Mateo County’s bay shoreline is at “Sea Change: San Mateo”
The future of Highway 37 is the focus of an ongoing research effort coordinated by Caltrans that involves UC Davis’s Road Ecology Center: Highway 37 Stewardship Study
A collection of last winter’s extreme high tides -- now known as King Tides -- is at Flickr account


The Embarcadero
For information on the current efforts to redo the Port of San Francisco’s waterfront land-use plan, view “Waterfront Plan Update”
A detailed study on changes along the bay waterfront since 1997 – from Fisherman’s Wharf to Pier 96 – is at “Waterfront Land Use Plan Review”
An updated edition of San Francisco State University Associate Professor Jasper Rubin’s “A Negotiated Landscape: The Transformation of San Francisco’s Waterfront Since 1950” will be published this summer by University of Pittsburgh Press.


Treasure Island
For information on Pier 70, visit the developer's website.
Additional information on Pier 70 can be found at the Port of San Francisco’s more exhaustive look at the area’s planning history.
The Mission Rock website was prepared by the San Francisco Giants for the 2015 ballot initiative to raise the allowable heights at Mission Rock.
More than a decade in the making, Treasure Island’s current planning vision can be explored here.
The redevelopment of Candlestick Point and the former Hunters Point Naval Shipyard is billed by its developer as The San Francisco Shipyard.
