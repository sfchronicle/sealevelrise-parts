The adaptation plan for Pier 70 also backs into higher ground, in this case the Dogpatch neighborhood. The plan is to regrade the 28-acre site, once part of a shipyard that employed as many as 18,000 people during World War II, so that it starts at the water and then angles up to building parcels located 66 inches above today’s average tide.

“It will be as if we’re tilting the site, like a tabletop,” said Jack Sylvan, developer Forest City’s project manager.

Which sounds simple, except that two historic industrial structures on low-lying ground are included in the larger redevelopment plans. A substation that dates to around 1900 will be moved to higher ground, the plan from the start. With the other building, an immense ghost of corrugated steel from 1941 where hulls were assembled, the preferred option is to jack up the gaunt frame and then restore the old building at the new elevation.

“We could leave it in a moat and build the soil up around it,” Sylvan said. “Sea level rise would never get to it, but you’d feel like the building was in a bathtub.”

The site’s transition from today’s shoreline to the planned development zone involves a concept called “managed retreat.” The slope would be terraced in a procession of paths and native plants, culminating in a landscaped strip above 2100 extreme projections and, behind it, the Bay Trail.

“This lets us provide for flexibility,” said Sylvan, who expects Forest City to start construction on the decade-long project in TKTK. “We want to design for today, but accomodate for what might happen in the future.”
